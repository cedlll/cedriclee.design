System · 01

Principles

Three rules the system answers to when a new component or screen needs designing.

01

Money is the loudest thing on the screen

Tabular numerals, 26px display weight, never wrapped. Everything else — chrome, labels, even brand — defers to the amount.

02

Status is a taxonomy, not a feeling

Four states. Same colors, same dot, same shape, same place in every row. Operators learn the language once.

03

Restraint is the brand

A dashboard people use during stressful end-of-week payouts shouldn't add visual noise. One brand color. One typeface. One shadow.

Decisions · 01

Notable tradeoffs

Five decisions worth defending. Each one was a fork in the road; the picked path and the rejected option are both logged.

Status palette

Muted semantic colors over saturated traffic-light

Status pills sit inside dense tables that operators stare at all day. Saturated red/yellow/green would hijack scanning and exhaust the eye. The chosen palette uses desaturated text colors (#A43C31, not #FF0000) plus a redundant dot — accessible without being aggressive. A later pass (documented below) dropped the tinted pill background entirely in favor of flat colored text, once the dot proved sufficient as the redundant channel.

Same payouts. Bright fills compete with peso amounts; flat colored text keeps the money column primary and the status column quiet. The dot is what survives grayscale — the text weight and dot presence still separate "needs attention" from "done."

Rejected: Material-style filled chips with bright fills. Why: meaning-by-color-only fails for color-blind users and grayscale exports.

Brand color

Deep forest green, not Filipino-fintech blue

Most local fintech (GCash, Maya, UnionBank, BPI) defaults to blue. A deep forest green (#20563A) signals money and stability without competing in the same visual space as the consumer wallets the merchants are paying out to. A soft mint (#86C9A5) is reserved for affordances on the dark sidebar — the brand mark and focus rings — never for status, which keeps its own separate palette.

Rejected: A safer navy. Why: indistinguishable from competitors in side-by-side product screenshots.

Sidebar contrast

Near-black sidebar against a near-white canvas

The product is operational, not exploratory. A dark sidebar acts as a persistent visual anchor — operators always know where they are even when scrolling through a 50-row table. The 6% white active state is intentionally quiet because the page title already tells you the active route.

Rejected: Light sidebar with brand-colored active state. Why: active state competes with status pills inside the work area.

Failed state

A pulsing dot, not a banner

Failed payouts need attention without disrupting the rest of the work. The Failed row's dot uses animate-pulse-dot — a quiet motion cue visible in peripheral vision that doesn't add a dismissable banner. Failed counts also surface as a dedicated KPI cell so the operator can triage from the top of the dashboard.

Rejected: A red toast or modal. Why: destination is the History view, not a one-time acknowledgment.

Time format

UTC timestamps with explicit "(UTC)" label

Disbursements cross banking cutoffs and clear on rails with their own timezones. A merchant in Manila and a partner in Singapore looking at the same row need to agree on what "Mar 28, 6:55 PM" means. Showing UTC explicitly is uglier than localized time but eliminates a class of reconciliation bugs.

Rejected: Local time with a tooltip showing UTC. Why: hover state is invisible on mobile, where ~40% of merchants check the dashboard.

Components · 01

Status pill

The most-loaded component in the product. Four states map to a fixed taxonomy. v1 shipped as a tinted background pill; v2 dropped the fill entirely — the label is now flat colored text, and a 6px solid dot is the sole redundant cue that survives color-blindness and grayscale screenshots. The Failed dot still pulses to draw attention without requiring a banner.

Components · 02

KPI metric card

The dashboard's primary container. v1 shipped as four separately shadowed cards, each with an icon badge. v2 merged them into one bordered "segmented card" — a 4-column grid with 1px hairline dividers between cells — and dropped the icon badges entirely: eyebrow label, value, and a flat trend/count readout in the footer are enough. The trend text ties back to the brand color, so positive movement reads as "on-brand" without needing a separate chart.

Components · 05

Data table

Tables are the operator's main workspace. Sentence-case headers (v1's uppercase/tracked headers were dropped for a quieter read), generous 14×20 cell padding, tabular numerals on amount columns, and right-aligned status pills create a consistent left-to-right read: who → when → how much → what state. v2 also added pagination — 50 rows load 10 at a time instead of one long scroll.
